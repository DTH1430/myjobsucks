Part 1: How I Got This Job In The First Place

So basically I was looking for a job in the industry, I don't really know what I should get into (probably web dev, I don't really know).

I spent days scrolling through job listings, each one requiring "5 years of experience in a framework that was released last Tuesday." You know the drill.

Then one day, a recruiter hit me up:

"Hey, we have a position in cybersecurity. Are you interested?"

And my dumb ass said "Sure, why not?"

Little did I know, this would be the beginning of my suffering. But hey, a job is a job, right? Wrong.

Part 2: The Interview That Should Have Been A Warning

The interview was... interesting. They asked me about firewalls, penetration testing, and something called "zero-trust architecture." I nodded along like I totally knew what they were talking about.

"Do you have experience with SIEM tools?"

"Absolutely," I lied, having Googled what SIEM stands for approximately 3 minutes before the call.

They hired me anyway. Either they were desperate, or my poker face was legendary. Probably both.

Part 3: Day One Reality Check

First day on the job. I walked in thinking I'd be hacking stuff like in the movies. You know, green text scrolling on a black screen, dramatic typing, maybe some sunglasses indoors.

Instead, I got:

  • 300 unread emails about password resets
  • A spreadsheet tracking "security incidents" (mostly people clicking phishing links)
  • A meeting about compliance that lasted 4 hours. FOUR. HOURS.
  • Someone asking me to fix their printer because "IT is IT, right?"

Where's the hacking? Where's the adrenaline? Where's the cool sunglasses moment?

Part 4: The Daily Grind (AKA My Personal Hell)

Here's what my typical day looks like:

  1. 8:00 AM
    Check alerts. 90% are false positives. The other 10% are Karen from accounting clicking on "You've won a free iPhone" for the 47th time.
  2. 10:00 AM
    Meeting about the last meeting.
  3. 11:00 AM
    Someone locked themselves out. Again.
  4. 12:00 PM
    Lunch. The only good part of the day.
  5. 1:00 PM
    Write a report that nobody will read.
  6. 3:00 PM
    Another meeting. This time about metrics. Kill me.
  7. 5:00 PM
    Go home, question life choices, repeat tomorrow.

Part 5: The Things Nobody Tells You About Security

  • You will be blamed for EVERYTHING.
    System down? Your fault. Got hacked? Your fault. Coffee machine broken? Somehow, also your fault.
  • Nobody takes security training seriously.
    You can explain phishing 1000 times, they will still click the damn link.
  • Compliance is 80% paperwork
    ...and 20% actually securing things.
  • The budget is always "next quarter."
    Spoiler: next quarter never comes.
  • You will develop trust issues.
    Everyone is a potential threat now.

Part 6: Why I'm Still Here (I Ask Myself This Daily)

Honestly? I have no clue.

Maybe it's the paycheck. Maybe it's the Stockholm syndrome. Maybe it's because learning web dev from scratch sounds exhausting and I'm already too deep in this hole.

Or maybe, just maybe, there's something satisfying about catching that one real threat among a thousand false alarms. Like finding a needle in a haystack, if the haystack was on fire and everyone was screaming.

But mostly it's the paycheck. Let's be real.

Part 7: What I Actually Wanted To Do

Before this security gig happened, I had dreams. Big dreams. Stupid dreams, probably.

  • Build cool websites that actually look good
  • Create web apps that people would use and enjoy
  • Write code that doesn't involve reading CVE reports at 2 AM
  • Maybe contribute to open source and feel like a real developer

Instead, I'm here writing policies about password complexity requirements. Living the dream.

The Brutally Honest Conclusion

So here I am, a security guy who would rather be building websites, writing about his life on a page with no CSS because apparently even adding styles feels like too much effort now.

Will I ever switch careers? Maybe. Probably. Eventually. When the burnout hits critical mass.

For now, I'll keep pretending I know what I'm doing, one false positive at a time.

If you're considering a career in security: Think twice. Then think again. Then maybe reconsider web dev. At least CSS won't make you paranoid about every email attachment.
